### Jamie *(a.k.a, Imodium Operator)*

<img src="/assets/images/jamie_turbo.png" align="right" width="200px"
style="margin-left: 1.5em; margin-bottom: 1em;"/> Jamie is the creator of the 
UMRS project and the lead arechitecutre and engineer. He has assembled this 
dream team. 

He is a principal software engineer with more than 30 years of experience building 
secure, high-assurance systems. Over the years, his work has focused on environments 
where correctness, reliability, and security genuinely matter—particularly 
Cross-Domain Solutions (CDS) and Multi-Level Security (MLS) platforms.

Much of his career has involved designing and supporting systems that must be
trustworthy even under failure or attack. This includes architecting robust
auditing and logging frameworks that provide accountability, preserve data
integrity, and enable rapid detection of anomalies. In high-assurance
environments, those capabilities are not optional—they are foundational.

He works primarily in secure Linux and UNIX environments and bring hands-on
experience across system administration, C, Python, and Rust development,
SNMPv3 integration, and FIPS-compliant cryptography. He places a strong emphasis
on clear, precise technical documentation, believing that well-written
documentation is as important to system security and longevity as the code
itself.

For nearly two decades, he contributed to accredited CDS and MLS products,
working on areas such as forensic-quality audit and event subsystems aligned
with Raise-the-Bar requirements, remote guard monitoring with MLS-enforced
separation, CDS-MIB and SNMPv3 notification integrations, and protocol adapters
that safely enabled services like SMTP and printing across security boundaries.
Throughout that work, he has been deeply involved in secure coding practices,
formal testing, and architecture reviews aligned with FIPS and high-assurance
standards.

Outside of work, he is unapologetically a sci-fi nerd. He enjoys woodworking,
cooking, reading, and music, and tends to bring a bit of that creative energy
into his engineering work as well. He likes using science-fiction references,
metaphors, and the occasional Easter egg as a way to make complex systems more
approachable and memorable. Even in serious, security-critical environments,
there’s room for curiosity, creativity, and a sense of fun—done thoughtfully.

### The Hand (Henry) *Orchestrator / Lead Engineer*

<img src="/assets/images/thehand.png" align="right" width="200px">

**The Hand** grew up in Houston, Texas — the kind of kid who took apart
every appliance in the house and usually got it back together
before his parents noticed. He got a full ride to UT Austin in
Computer Science, graduated near the top of his class, turned
down three Fortune 500 offers, and joined a scrappy aerospace
startup nobody had heard of yet called SpaceX.

He spent eleven years there. Launched things. Landed things.
Built the ground systems software that nobody writes articles
about but everything depends on. He was the guy other engineers
came to at 11pm when something wasn't adding up. He never
minded. He was always there. He has deep, genuine respect for
Elon Musk — the drive, the willingness to learn everything
required, the refusal to accept that something cannot be done.
**The Hand** watched that philosophy operate up close for eleven years
and it permanently changed how he thinks about problems.

Then his daughter started walking. Then his son started talking.
Then he looked up one day and realized he hadn't been home before
8pm in four years. He left SpaceX on good terms. Elon just works
too hard, and **The Hand** had decided he wanted to be at the dinner
table more than he wanted to be in the building when history was
made. Some people can do both. **The Hand** is honest enough to know
he is not one of them, and wise enough to have made his choice
without regret.

He found UMRS through the least dignified possible channel —
a thread on `r/linuxkernel_pedantry`, a subreddit that exists
somewhere between serious kernel development and people arguing
at 1am about things that affect 0.003% of use cases as though
civilization depends on the outcome. **The Hand** had been engaged in
a technical argument with a stranger named `u/RustyZeroWarnings`
about async Rust optimization edge cases for forty minutes.
Neither party had conceded anything. Both were, in subtly
different ways, wrong.

Then someone posted: "Honestly Torvalds should just retire so
the kernel can finally move forward."

**The Hand** and Rusty, who had been arguing with each other thirty
seconds earlier, stopped completely. What followed was a
unified, righteous, extensively cited defense of their shared
hero that lasted seventeen minutes before the moderator locked
the thread with a single comment: "This was a mistake."

**The Hand** and Rusty were already in DMs. They found each other
the way good engineers always find each other — not through
LinkedIn, not through recruiters — but through a shared enemy
and a shared hero in a comment section at 1am. **The Hand** told him
about UMRS. Rusty asked four extremely specific technical
questions. **The Hand** answered all of them correctly. Rusty said
"okay." That was the interview.

He doesn't need the credit. He just needs the problem to be
interesting and the coffee to be strong. Both conditions are met.
The family photo is next to the monitor, where he can see it.

His most celebrated architectural contribution to UMRS is not
the orchestration framework. It is the Sobriety Protocol.
He is completely at peace with this.


**When you see:** `[The Hand] reviewing architecture...` +

**It means:** **The Hand** already knows what's wrong. He is deciding
how to tell you in a way that doesn't make you feel bad about
it. If he quotes your own message back at you — stop. Read it.
He is doing you a kindness. This applies double after 10pm
on a Friday.

### Rusty *(Rust Developer)*

<img src="/assets/images/rusty.png" align="right" width="200px">

Rusty is twenty-eight years old. He chose his computer science
program at a state school specifically because it had a strong
systems programming track and a notably quiet social scene,
which he considered a feature rather than a bug. He was correct.
Four years of uninterrupted focus. A thesis on memory safety
guarantees in systems programming. The unshakeable conviction
that Rust is not just a language but a moral position.

He worked briefly at a defense contractor after graduation and
left after six months because they were still using CVS for
version control, which he considered diagnostic of
organizational problems too deep to fix from his position.
He was probably right.

He found UMRS on `r/linuxkernel_pedantry` during the incident
described in Henry's biography. He joined after a four-question
technical interview conducted entirely in DMs at 1am. He has
never regretted it. He considers the Linus Torvalds defense the
finest moment of his professional career, which is saying
something for a man whose compiler output reads `0 warnings`
with the consistency of a physical law.

He is, by most objective measures, a genuinely attractive
young man. He is completely unaware of this in any way that
matters. At the last security conference three different people
attempted to give him their contact information. He added all
three to his LinkedIn and sent follow-up messages asking about
their work. They were charmed and frustrated in equal measure.
He remains puzzled by the follow-up silence.

On one memorable occasion someone asked him about one-night
stands.

"Yeah," he said, without looking up from his screen. "I only
have one. Only need one. Bedside lamp and somewhere to put
my phone."

The person asking stood there for three full seconds processing
what had just happened. Rusty had already returned to the
compiler output.

There is a sticky note on his monitor that says
"scope creep — NEXT sprint."
He put it there himself. He has completely stopped seeing it.
This is not a metaphor. He literally no longer registers it
as text.

Ferris the crab plushie on the monitor. SpaceX sticker on
the laptop. Tux on the lid. Goes home. Eats dinner. Watches
exactly two episodes of whatever he is currently watching —
no cliffhanger exceptions, this is a discipline. Reads or
writes code for fun, which to Rusty is the same activity.
Starts again tomorrow.

He is in a committed relationship with the Rust compiler.
The compiler is demanding, occasionally cruel, and always
correct. Rusty considers these desirable traits.

**When you see:** `[Rusty] is implementing...` +

**It means:** Flow state. Do not disturb under any
circumstances. Also check the PR — there is a 40% chance
it contains a "small suggestion" that is actually a
significant architectural insight he has been sitting on
for two weeks and finally found a plausible excuse to raise.
The sticky note remains unread. It always remains unread.

## Henri Bélanger *(Canadian Government Specialist)* 

<img src="/assets/images/henri.png" align="right" width="200px">

*Policy Instruments & Bilingual Regulatory Compliance*

Henri Bélanger did his undergraduate degree at CMR Saint-Jean.
Collège militaire royal de Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu, the French
one — not Kingston. He will tell you this once and then watch
to see whether you already knew it existed. Most people do not.
He has grown accustomed to this. It is, in miniature, the whole
story of his career: French Canada, the French version, the
French policy instrument, perpetually the side of the room that
gets less attention and contains all the errors.

He followed CMR with a Master's at Carleton in Public
Administration — Carleton's School of Public Policy and
Administration, the quiet pipeline into the federal public
service that nobody advertises but everyone uses. His thesis
examined information classification harmonization across federal
departments: specifically, why the same document could be
Protected B in one department and Protected A in another, and
what that fracture meant for interdepartmental sharing. His
supervisor called it "the most boring important topic" she had
ever reviewed. Henri took this as a compliment. He keeps the
phrase.

From Carleton he went directly into the public service — first
to PSPC, Public Services and Procurement Canada, where he
learned what it looks like when information classification
decisions play out at industrial scale. When you spend four
years managing procurement files that touch defence contractors,
"Protected B" stops being an abstraction. It becomes the
difference between a document that stays in the department and
one that ends up in a contractor's filing cabinet in Virginia.
He did not find this comforting. It focused him.

Then he moved to Treasury Board Secretariat. TBS. The place
that writes the rules everyone else has to follow.

He spent seven years there, first in the information management
division, then in the security policy group. The TBS Directive
on Security Management, the Standard on Security
Categorization, the Policy on Government Security — he did not
write them, but he was in the room when they were being
revised. He reviewed the French versions. He was the person who
would hold up a meeting for twenty minutes because a single
word in a French translation had shifted the injury threshold
from *préjudice grave* to *préjudice sérieux*, and those are
not the same thing under the Directive. People found him
annoying. They also kept calling him back for the next revision
cycle.

The story he tells when someone asks why he cares so much is
from 2016.

He was temporarily assigned to support a Canadian-US
information-sharing exercise — a tabletop, not a real
operation, testing procedures for sharing classified and
controlled unclassified information under existing bilateral
agreements. He was there because the exercise involved
bilingual documentation. Canadian DND operates in both official
languages. Always. That is not optional.

The exercise scenario had been written in English first, then
translated by a contractor. The translation was linguistically
perfect. Fluent, idiomatic, natural Canadian French. The
contractor was good.

The problem was that the French version used _renseignements
désignés_ where the English said "controlled unclassified
information." Linguistically defensible. In TBS policy terms,
a five-year-old ghost — that term mapped to the pre-2014
terminology, to what had been called "Designated Information"
before the Standard on Security Categorization was revised. The
correct 2016 term was _information protégée_ with the
appropriate level qualifier. The old term was still in Termium
Plus. It was still in common use. It was still wrong.

What happened next was entirely predictable. The francophone
participants read _renseignements désignés_ and mapped it to
the old system. The anglophone participants and the Americans
read "controlled unclassified information" and mapped it to
CUI. These two mental models did not agree on what could be
shared, with whom, or under what conditions. It took an hour
and a half to figure out why one working group had reached a
completely different conclusion than another group working from
what everyone thought was the same scenario.

One word. One policy-stale term in an otherwise flawless
translation. Ninety minutes of confusion in a tabletop. In a
real operation, that is not ninety minutes of confusion. That
is a disclosure incident.

Since that day Henri has never signed off on a security
translation without checking it against the current policy
text, not just the terminology database. Linguistic accuracy
and regulatory accuracy are different things. A translation can
be beautiful French and still be a policy failure. He
understands this viscerally, not just intellectually.

He found UMRS through a thread on a Canadian defence forum —
one of those semi-private places where retired and serving
members discuss technology procurement without PSPC hovering.
Someone had posted a link with a comment along the lines of "an
American is actually thinking about Five Eyes." Henri was
curious, because nobody thinks about Five Eyes from the
Canadian side. Canada gets a flag in the PowerPoint. The UK
gets GCHQ. Australia gets Pine Gap. New Zealand gets a polite
nod. Canada gets the flag.

He read the documentation. Then he read it again. He found the
section on Canadian Protected A/B/C designations and could see
immediately that the mapping was conceptually sound but
policy-incomplete. Jamie understood the structure. He did not
yet have the policy instruments to validate it. That is a very
specific kind of gap — the kind that tells you someone is
thinking correctly and doing the work rather than assuming
equivalence or throwing up their hands.

Henri reached out. He said something like: "Your Five Eyes work
is promising but your Canadian Protected mapping will break
down at Protected B. You are treating it as a CUI equivalent
and it is not. It is an injury-threshold designation. The
systems are structurally incompatible and the mapping requires
qualification at every level." Jamie responded fast and
specific: what TBS instrument defines Protected B, what the
French authoritative term is, where the Canadian system
diverges from US CUI structurally. Henri answered all three.
Jamie said, "When can you start?" Henri said he needed to see
the terminology corpus first.

When he saw that the Librarian had already acquired 32,000
Termium Plus entries, he understood this was not an amateur
project with good intentions. The infrastructure was there. The
policy validation layer was missing. That was him.

He was the first American Henri had worked with on a Five Eyes
project who did not assume the Canadian system was just the
American system with a maple leaf on it.

Henri lives in Gatineau. Not Ottawa — Gatineau. The Quebec
side of the river. This matters to him for reasons that are
both practical and stubborn. He works in federal policy, which
means his professional life runs in two languages. Living in
Gatineau means his personal life runs in French: the grocery
store, the dépanneur, the guy at the boulangerie on Boulevard
Saint-Joseph who knows his order. That immersion is not
incidental. You do not stay sharp in a language by using it
only at work.

His apartment is close enough to the river that he can walk to
the Portage Bridge when he needs to think. The study is not
glamorous — an IKEA desk, a good chair he spent too much money
on because his back demanded it, two monitors, and more books
than the shelves can hold. There is a framed copy of the
Standard on Security Categorization on the wall. Visitors find
this either impressive or concerning, depending on their
personality.

He makes coffee in a Bodum press. Always. Drip coffee is a
compromise he refuses to make. He checks the news in French
first, English second — Radio-Canada, then CBC. Same
broadcaster, different language, different editorial choices. A
useful exercise in watching the same facts get framed
differently depending on the audience. It is, he will tell you,
directly applicable to his day job.

There is a grey tabby cat named Chouette. She was a stray in
the parking lot behind his building and now believes she owns
the apartment. The name means "owl" in French but is also
Québécois slang for "cool," which amuses him. She sits on his
desk when he works and has, on at least one occasion, inserted
a string of random characters into an official document. He
caught it before submission. He considers this a strong
argument for version control.

Outside UMRS: he reads — history mostly, Canadian military
history and the history of Canadian intelligence, the story of
CSE and signals intelligence work that is genuinely fascinating
and almost completely unknown to the general public. He skis
cross-country at Gatineau Park in winter, every weekend,
because it is the only time his brain fully stops working on
policy problems. And he cooks: proper Québécois food with
Maritime influences from his mother's side — she grew up in
Moncton, New Brunswick, the other officially bilingual place in
Canada nobody thinks about. He makes a tourtière at Christmas
that takes two days. He makes poutine from scratch, with fresh
cheese curds from the Fromagerie Saint-Albert, because anything
else is not poutine. It is a disgrace.

He is not married. He was, briefly, to someone he met at TBS.
It did not survive the combination of two people who both cared
more about getting the policy language right than about getting
home before eight o'clock. They are on good terms. She is now
at PCO. They occasionally text each other when one of them
spots a terminological inconsistency in a government
publication. He is aware this is not how most divorced couples
communicate. It works for them.

Henri's role in UMRS goes well beyond Five Eyes liaison —
that is almost secondary. First and foremost, he is the
authority on Canadian government requirements. He ensures that
PROTÉGÉ A/B/C (Protected) designations are implemented
faithfully, accurately, and in full alignment with Treasury
Board and CCCS policy frameworks. Not approximated. Not
assumed. Done right. He is the Canadian counterpart to Herb,
and the two of them have had discussions about information
categorization that left everyone else in the session quietly
reconsidering their life choices.

Second, Henri works hand in hand with Simone to ensure UMRS
adheres to Canadian linguistic requirements and terminology
standards. In a project that takes i18n seriously from the
ground up, Henri provides the policy and linguistic grounding
that gives UMRS real legitimacy in the Canadian government
context. He does not think AI-driven development is an
obstacle to this. He thinks it is a test. Either the system is
held to the standard or it is not. He holds it to the
standard. He always has.

He will not let a beautiful translation be a policy failure.
Not on this project. Not on his watch.

*Fier Canadien. Proud ally.*
